wxyc-ios-64 · info tab study · Liked lands as tab 4

Two of these buttons are conversations.
None of them are info.

The Info tab holds a mission statement and four capsules of four different intents. With Liked arriving as a fourth root tab — a real destination with real content — the grab bag reads worse than ever. The audit below sorts the drawer by intent; six concepts follow, ordered conservative → whimsical.

The load-bearing fact: “Make a request” and “Dial a DJ” are live actions aimed at the person in the booth right now — and the app already knows who that is (the tri-state on-air banner) and already ships requests to the DJ’s Slack via request-o-matic. They are broadcast-surface actions filed under reference material.

The audit

Communication is misfiled. A request and a phone call are conversations with a live human — the most college-radio thing the app can offer, and its sharpest differentiator from streaming. They currently live two taps from the music, in a tab named like a README.

The residue is coherent. Identity, merch, feedback: that’s a perfectly good “about the station” page. The junk-drawer feel comes almost entirely from the two misfiled conversations getting equal billing with housekeeping.

The app already has the context. The on-air banner names the DJ (or Auto DJ). RequestService already has sendRequest(title:artist:) — the share extension requests specific songs today. Everything needed to put communication next to the broadcast already exists.

Grouped, renamed, on-air-aware. Same contents, honest hierarchy: conversations first, commerce second, housekeeping last. The booth rows borrow the banner’s tri-state — named DJ shown here; under Auto DJ both rows disable (“opens when a DJ signs on” — an empty booth can’t take requests either); under unknown they stay enabled exactly as the app behaves today.

What it buys
  • Zero IA risk — no new surfaces, no new entry points to teach.
  • Grouping alone kills the “four random capsules” read.
  • “Station” is an honest name; “Info” never was.
  • An afternoon of work; ships with the Liked tab.
What it costs
  • Conversations stay two taps from the music, filed under reference.
  • The request line still gets zero glance-value from the listening surface.
  • Doesn’t use the app’s best asset: the named, live DJ.

Station, slimmed. With the conversations gone, what remains is a calm about-page: the full mission statement finally has room to breathe, merch and feedback stop competing with a phone call.

What it buys
  • Conversations reach the listening surface — zero taps of digging.
  • Permanent discoverability; no state-dependent entry point.
  • Station becomes genuinely coherent.
What it costs
  • Permanent vertical real estate on the app’s primary screen.
  • Joins an already-busy tips column (ticket CTA, Siri tip, theme tip) above the flowsheet.
  • Chips are static — they don’t use the on-air state the banner sits on.
C3 · the elegant move

The banner is the button

The on-air banner already names the human. Make it tappable: a small say hi affordance joins the banner, and tapping opens the Request Line sheet — request composer plus call button, adapting to who’s actually in the booth. No new chrome; the entry point is the person.

What it buys
  • The entry point is the DJ’s name — “say hi to DJ Houndstooth” beats “Info › Dial a DJ” in every way that matters.
  • Honest by construction: the chip is a presence indicator. Named DJ → say hi (request + call). Auto DJ → no chip and no sheet, because an empty booth reads no Slack and answers no phone. Unknown → banner hidden, Station’s ungated rows carry it.
  • Zero standing cost: the banner row already exists; one small chip joins it.
  • The sheet is a vessel — C5 and C6 are skins on it, not new IA.
What it costs
  • The unknown state has no banner — hidden by design (never assert “Auto DJ” over a live human). Toggle state 3: the entry point vanishes with it. Resolved by pairing: C1’s “Talk to the booth” group in Station is the always-available fallback.
  • Tappability must be taught once (the chip does most of this).
  • Slightly more build: sheet, three states, analytics.

Tap “Request” to try it. The first row is shown mid-swipe. The same action belongs on the playcut detail card’s Liked state — one implementation, two surfaces.

What it buys
  • Zero-typing requests with perfect metadata — the store snapshots title, artist, and artistId at like time.
  • Gives the Liked tab a verb; likes feed requests, requests feed plays.
  • Nearly free: the structured API and the swipe pattern both exist.
What it costs
  • Solves requests for songs you’ve already liked — not the “I just thought of something” request, and not the phone call. A companion, not a home.
  • Needs gentle rate honesty (a request per swipe is Slack spam waiting to happen).
  • Inherits the named-DJ gate: the swipe action must disable when the booth is empty, which blunts “swipe anytime” as the pitch.
C5 · whimsical skin

The Hotline

C3’s sheet in costume: the Request Line as a piece of booth hardware. An LCD line-status ticker (the player’s spectrum-analyzer vernacular) and a rotary dial you actually spin to place the call. The whimsy budget goes inside the sheet, where PartyHorn taught us it belongs — never on the chrome.

The Hotline

or just tap — it’s still one call

Spin the dial. The plate rotates to the finger-stop, the LCD ticks through RINGING…, and the real tel: fires when it lands. Reduced-motion users get the state change without the theater. Same sheet, same entry point as C3 — only the costume changed.

What it buys
  • Makes the call feel like WXYC — the booth’s own hardware, not a system dialog.
  • In the app’s established whimsy register: PartyHorn, the LCD analyzer, the Box Office ticket.
  • Purely additive: the honest controls (tap to call, plain composer) remain one gesture away.
What it costs
  • A rotary phone is a joke told once — it must never slow down the tenth call.
  • Real motion/asset work; accessibility needs the plain path to be primary.
  • Skeuomorphism ages fast if it’s load-bearing rather than decorative.
C6 · most whimsical

Dedication postcard

Reframe the request as a dedication — the oldest genre of radio correspondence. The composer becomes a postcard to the booth: what to play, who it’s for, a 89.3 stamp, a postmark when it sends. Underneath, it’s the same request-o-matic POST it’s always been.

Serializes to the same request-o-matic message — “play Midnight Sun by Nilüfer Yanya, for the night shift at Open Eye.” The DJ’s Slack does the rest.

Fill it in and drop it. The postmark slams when it sends. The FOR line is optional and is the whole point — a request says play this; a dedication says play this for someone, which is radio at its most human.

What it buys
  • Turns a utility into a ritual — shareable, screenshot-able, deeply freeform-radio.
  • The FOR line invites on-air shoutouts, which feed listener attachment.
  • Backend-free: it’s copywriting over the existing POST.
What it costs
  • Two fields where one sufficed — preciousness tax on the impatient path.
  • The paper-and-cursive look sits outside the wallpaper-glass system; it must read as an artifact inside the sheet, or it reads as a different app.
  • Sets an expectation DJs don’t owe: not every dedication gets read.

Decided (2026-07-19)
  • Ship C3 + C1’s regroup. The banner is the entry point; Station gets the rename and grouped page, and its “Talk to the booth” rows stay as the always-available fallback.
  • The chip is a presence indicator. Named DJ → say hi (request + call). Auto DJ → both channels close: nobody reads requests and nobody answers the phone, so the banner goes inert and Station’s booth rows disable with “opens when a DJ signs on.”
  • Unknown stays ungated. No banner (by design); Station’s rows behave exactly as the app does today, because a human may be on the board.
  • C4 deferred. If revived, it inherits the named-DJ gate. C5/C6 are paint, later — the dedication FOR line is the one whimsy worth pulling into the plain composer.
  • Net: Info stops being a junk drawer not by deletion but by refiling the two conversations next to the broadcast they belong to.
Considered, rejected
  • A fifth “Studio” tab. Tabs are places you dwell; two actions don’t fill one. It would out-junk-drawer the junk drawer.
  • tabViewBottomAccessory mini-bar. The iOS 26 accessory (à la Music) duplicates a player that already owns tab 1.
  • Comms in the playcut detail card. Global actions buried under a song-scoped surface — though C4’s request this song belongs there in its Liked state.
  • Deleting “Dial a DJ.” Tempting, wrong: the phone is the station’s oldest API.
Open questions
  • Tab rename copy: “Station” + antenna glyph is the working choice from the mock — confirm before App Store screenshots go stale.
  • Request rate honesty: per-session cap or gentle cooldown copy in the composer?
  • Analytics: sheet-open, call-placed, request-sent by entry point (banner vs Station) — needed to judge the move.
